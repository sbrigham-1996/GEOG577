Project Plan on Gaza

1. Objective/Question/Hypothesis

Map and quantify the extent of damage and destruction in Gaza by analyzing three key urban areas—Gaza City, Khan Yunis, and Rafah—during the Operation Protective Edge (2014) war and the 2023–present conflict using all applicable remote sensing techniques.

The extent and intensity of destruction in the Gaza Strip has significantly increased in the 2023–present conflict compared to Operation Protective Edge (2014) 50 days of war. The objective is to quantify and map the extent of damage and observe how it has changed over time in the three urban centers to understand urban change and recovery patterns.

2. Description of Area of Interest and Time Frame

Area of Interest: Gaza Strip, focusing on Gaza City, Khan Yunis, and Rafah.

Time-frame: Historical Comparison: Operation Protective Edge (2014) and the recent conflict since October 7, 2023.

3. Description of Data and Resolutions

Data Characteristics

Spatial Resolution:

Optical: Sentinel-2 (10m) and Landsat (30m) for broad land use change; SkyWatch High-Resolution (Airbus-Pleiades Neo HD15, Maxar) with 15–150cm resolution for detailed damage assessment; Thermal (Landsat TIRS) for detecting heat anomalies from fires or explosions. SAR: Sentinel-1 (10m) for detecting structural changes and damage patterns; DEM Data (SRTM, possibly ASTER) for understanding elevation and terrain changes; SkyWatch SAR Data (ICEYE) for high-resolution structural damage detection.

Temporal Resolution:

Operation Protective Edge (2014): Pre-Event: July 8, 2014 – August 26, 2014; Post-Event: August 26, 2014 – October 31, 2014 2023 Conflict: Pre-Event: September 15, 2023 – October 7, 2023; Post-Event: October 7, 2023 – November 15, 2023 (Immediate post-conflict) November 15, 2023 – May 15, 2024 (Short- to mid-term recovery) May 15, 2024 – March 7, 2025 (Longer-term reconstruction phase)

Spectral Resolution: Multispectral/Hyperspectral data (Sentinel-2, Landsat) to analyze burn scars and vegetation loss; SAR data (Sentinel-1, SkyWatch) to detect structural damage; Thermal (Landsat TIRS) for heat anomalies; DEM data (SRTM) for elevation changes.

4. Ancillary Data

HUMDATA Roads Dataset: https://data.humdata.org/dataset/hotosm_pse_roads Population Density: Data available at WorldPop. Infrastructure Data: Accessible via HUMDATA and OpenStreetMap. Displacement: Extract coordinates for refugee camps from Wikipedia. Buildings: Acquire locations of schools, hospitals, and other facilities for overlay as vectors.

5. Data Availability

Sentinel-1 & Sentinel-2: Available via Google Earth Engine and Copernicus. Landsat 8/9: Available via Google Earth Engine and USGS. DEMs (SRTM, ASTER): Available from NASA, USGS, and Google Earth Engine. Population Density: Available from WorldPop and USGS. Infrastructure Data: Freely available through HUMDATA. Displacement Data: Extract coordinates from sources like Wikipedia. High-Resolution Commercial Data: Requires commercial approval; may be accessible via SkyWatch and Google Earth Engine.

Data Limitations: Sentinel-2’s 10m resolution may not capture fine-scale destruction; optical data can be affected by clouds; Landsat’s 30m resolution may not detect individual building damage; capturing population movements solely via remote sensing is challenging.
